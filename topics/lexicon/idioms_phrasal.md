# Idioms and phrasal expressions in Yerevan colloquial speech

Yerevan colloquial Armenian is **idiom-dense**. Ghamoyan's Chapter 3
catalogues fixed expressions across multiple semantic fields. The
typology that emerges:

1. **Single-noun idiom families** — one core word generating many
   phrasal idioms (the dog/շուն set is the canonical case). [#1]
2. **Synonymous idiom clusters** — multiple distinct idioms
   converging on a single meaning (the "be silent" cluster). [#2]
3. **Antithetical pairs** — antonymic idiom-pairs sharing a body-part
   metaphor (եye / hand / tongue). [#3]

Plus phrasal jargon verbs (`կուտ տալ`, `ձև բռնել`, `յուղ վառել`,
`կոշկվել`, etc.) covered in `topics/lexicon/yerevan_slang.md`.

## Pattern 1: single-noun idiom families (the dog set)

The noun `շուն` "dog" generates a productive family of idioms — at
least 7 distinct phrasal expressions documented by ghamoyan: [#1]

| idiom | literal | meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Շան բախտ ունի | "has dog's luck" | (s)he is unlucky |
| Շան օրի ա | "is having a dog's day" | (s)he is in a terrible state |
| Շան էրես ունի | "has a dog's face" | shameless |
| Շան քթից ջուր ա խմում | "drinks water from a dog's nose" | severely overworked |
| Շան հոտառություն ունի | "has a dog's nose" | (positive) excellent perception |
| Շան լեզու ունի | "has a dog's tongue" | sharp / cutting speaker |
| Շունը տիրոջը չի ճանաչում | "the dog doesn't recognise its owner" | (of a crowded place) chaotic |

Plus diminutive variants (`շան թուլա`, `շան լակոտ`, `շան ձագ`)
which carry either *vulgar* or *light reproachful/intimate*
register depending on context. [#1]

Note the **register of "Շան բախտ ունի"**: in English "lucky dog" is
positive; the Armenian idiom is *the opposite* (unlucky). A direct
calque misleads. This is exactly the kind of lexical trap that AI
trained on English-Armenian parallel corpora can mishandle.

## Pattern 2: synonymous idiom clusters (silence)

The meaning **"to be silent / not speak"** is expressed by multiple
body-part idioms: [#2]

| idiom | literal | comment |
|-------|---------|---------|
| բերանը կապել | "tie one's mouth" | most direct |
| լեզուն փորը քաշել | "pull tongue into stomach" | colloquial |
| լեզուն ծակը կոխել | "shove tongue into hole" | (vulgar marking by ghamoyan) |
| լեզուն կտրվել | "tongue cuts off" | speaker-passive |
| ձայնը փորն ընկնել | "voice falls into stomach" | speaker-passive |

Five idioms for one core meaning. The choice between them carries
register information — a speaker selecting `լեզուն ծակը կոխել` vs
`բերանը կապել` is signaling something about their stance toward the
addressee.

## Pattern 3: antithetical body-part pairs

A productive idiom-formation pattern: take a body part + adjectival
particle (`open` / `closed`, `out` / `in`), generate antonymic
idiom-pairs. [#3]

| dimension | "positive" pole | "negative" pole |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| eye + door | աչքը դռանը "eye on the door" (= faithful, waiting) | աչքը դուրս "eye out" (= unfaithful) |
| hand + open/closed | ձեռքը բաց "open hand" (= generous) | ձեռքը փակ "closed hand" (= miserly) |
| eye + valuation | աչքի լույս "eye-light" (= beloved) | աչքի գրող "eye-scribe" (= hated) |
| God + animal/element | Աստծու գառ "God's lamb" (= calm) | Աստծու կրակ "God's fire" (= wild) |

The structural symmetry is **pedagogically useful**: learning one
member of the pair makes the antonym partly predictable.

## Other phrasal idioms (selected)

From the same ghamoyan p68 area: [#2]

| idiom | literal | meaning |
|-------|---------|---------|
| պատին դեմ անել | "press against wall" | corner someone |
| կաշին փրկել | "save one's skin" | escape responsibility |
| ջուր ծեծել | "beat water" | do pointless work |
| յուղ վառել | "burn oil" | talk nonsense |
| ոտքերը լիզել | "lick someone's feet" | flatter excessively |
| ասեղների վրա նստել | "sit on needles" | be on edge |
| մի գնդակով երկու նապաստակ որսալ | "catch two hares with one bullet" | kill two birds with one stone |
| մի ձեռքով երկու ձմերուկ բռնել | "hold two watermelons in one hand" | attempt the impossible |

The last two are interesting because they have **direct counterparts
in Russian** (одним выстрелом двух зайцев убить, погнаться за двумя
зайцами) and English (kill two birds with one stone) — likely shared
through Russian or pan-cultural parallel.

## What this topic is *not*

- **Curses, blessings, oaths** (p46: `Հերդ էլ անիծած`, `Հողեմ
  գլուխդ`, `Ապրի արևդ`, `Տա Աստված`) are a distinct sub-category;
  they share the fixed-expression structure but carry illocutionary
  force (cursing/blessing) rather than ideational content.
  Worth a separate topic.
- **Pure proverbs** (full sentences with self-contained meaning,
  e.g. `Փոս փորողն ինքն ա ընկնում փոսը` "the one who digs a hole
  falls into it") are a related but distinct genre.
- **Phrasal jargon verbs** — covered in
  `topics/lexicon/yerevan_slang.md` (Pattern A: existing words with
  new metaphorical meanings) and the foreign-borrowing patterns in
  `topics/lexicon/code_switching_with_russian.md`.

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: idioms don't translate. Period. Don't try to
back-derive `Շան բախտ ունի` from "lucky dog" — they're opposite.
Memorise idioms as whole units with their meaning, not by parsing
the morphology. The body-part metaphors (eye = attention, hand =
generosity, tongue = speech) are pan-cultural enough to give you
weak intuition; the *direction* of the metaphor (eye on door =
faithful, eye out = unfaithful) is Armenian-specific.

**For a Russian L1**: Russian shares many idiom *patterns* with
Armenian (одним выстрелом / մի գնդակով; держать язык за зубами /
բերանը կապել). When in doubt, try the Russian idiom literally
translated — there's a non-trivial chance it works. But ghamoyan's
`Շան բախտ ունի` (= unlucky) is the opposite of *Везёт как утопленнику*
(= unlucky) which is itself ironic — the Armenian has the same ironic
direction but uses a different animal. Calibrate carefully.
