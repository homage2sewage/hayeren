# Subjunctive mood (future + past)

Armenian's subjunctive mood expresses **non-factual** actions —
potential, imaginary, conditional, or optional. [#1] Unlike the
indicative (which makes claims about reality), the subjunctive
suspends commitment to what's actually the case. It has two
paradigms — subjunctive future and subjunctive past — both
synthetic.

## Source

> **Sakayan [#1]**: "Unlike the indicative mood, which denotes
> certainty and factuality, the subjunctive mood expresses
> potential, imaginary, conditional, or optional actions. In
> Armenian, the subjunctive mood has two basic paradigms: a) the
> subjunctive future and b) the subjunctive past."

## Subjunctive future

Built from the infinitive stem + class-conditioned personal endings:

| 1st conj (-ել) | 2nd conj (-ալ) |
|----------------|----------------|
| գր**եմ** | կարդ**ամ** "(I) might / would write/read" |
| գր**ես** | կարդ**աս** |
| գր**ի** | կարդ**ա** |
| գր**ենք** | կարդ**անք** |
| գր**եք** | կարդ**աք** |
| գր**են** | կարդ**ան** |

Note: the 3sg form of 1st-conj subjunctive future (`գրի`) is
*identical* to the **negative participle** form (cf. `չեմ գրի`
"I won't write" — see `topics/morphology/negation.md`). The
homophony is a structural feature.

[#2]

## Subjunctive past

Built from the infinitive stem + different personal endings:

| 1st conj (-ել) | 2nd conj (-ալ) |
|----------------|----------------|
| գր**եի** | կարդ**այի** "(I) would have written / read" |
| գր**եիր** | կարդ**այիր** |
| գր**եր** | կարդ**ար** |
| գր**եինք** | կարդ**այինք** |
| գր**եիք** | կարդ**այիք** |
| գր**եին** | կարդ**ային** |

[#3]

## When the subjunctive shows up

Three main contexts:

1. **Subordinate clauses** introduced by certain conjunctions —
   `որ` "that", `որպեսզի` "in order that", `եթե` "if" (in
   counterfactuals), `գուցե` "perhaps":
   - *Ուզում եմ որ գա:* "I want him to come."
   - *Եթե գար, կտեսնեիք:* "If he came, you'd see."
2. **Polite requests / suggestions**:
   - *Կարո՞ղ եմ ասել:* "May I say [something]?"
3. **Input to derived moods** — hypothetical (`կ-` prefix),
   conditional, mandative (`պետք է / պիտի` + subj).

## Subjunctive vs indicative future

Armenian has *both* subjunctive future and indicative future
(`գրելու եմ` / future imperfect, see
`topics/morphology/future_tenses.md`). The functional split:

| form | construction | reading |
|------|--------------|---------|
| գրեմ | bare-stem subjunctive | "(I) might write / would write [non-committal]" |
| կգրեմ | `կ-` + subjunctive | "(I) will write [with determination]" — hypothetical future I |
| գրելու եմ | future participle + aux | "I will write / am going to write" — indicative future |

The hypothetical (`կգրեմ`) is the **most common** way to express
future in spoken Armenian; the indicative future (`գրելու եմ`) is
slightly more formal/precise.

## Negation

Pattern 1 from `topics/morphology/negation.md`: `չ-` prefix on the
synthetic form. No auxiliary movement (no auxiliary involved).

| affirmative | negative |
|-------------|----------|
| գրեմ | չգրեմ |
| գրեի | չգրեի |

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: English's subjunctive is largely vestigial
(`if I were`, `God save the queen`). Armenian's subjunctive is
*structurally* full and *semantically* central — it's used wherever
non-factuality is at stake. The closest English mental model is the
modal auxiliaries (`would, could, might, should`) collapsed into a
single mood. When you see "if + subjunctive past" in Armenian,
think "if I were to..." — counterfactual conditional.

**For a Russian L1**: Russian's *сослагательное наклонение*
(conditional/subjunctive) is formed by past tense + `бы`
(`написал бы` "would write"). Armenian's subjunctive is
*synthetic* (one word) where Russian's is *analytic* (two words).
The semantic range overlaps strongly — "potential, hypothetical,
counterfactual" — but Armenian's pre-`կ-` subjunctive future also
covers what Russian expresses with simple future plus context. The
mental model "irrealis mood, separate paradigm" maps directly.
