# Learning Armenian Vocabulary — Research & Resources

## The core problem

Hacking through texts builds **passive recognition** — your brain learns to decode meaning from context, but the words never become available for active production. That's why reading feels fine and speaking doesn't. Fixing this requires deliberate vocabulary work plus productive practice.

---

## What the research says

### Word cards beat word lists
A research synthesis on vocabulary learning found word cards lead to "relatively large gains" compared to studying word lists. Word cards are:
- **Focused** — attention goes to unknown words
- **Efficient** — large numbers of words can be learned quickly
- **Effective** — usable for both receptive (recognize) and productive (recall) learning

### Spaced repetition is the multiplier
The spacing effect (Ebbinghaus, 1885) shows we retain more when reviews are spread over time vs. crammed. Modern spaced repetition software (SRS) like Anki implements this automatically:
- Easy cards → shown rarely
- Hard cards → shown often
- Intervals expand each time you successfully recall (3 days → 15 days → 45 days → ...)

This brings vocabulary from short-term to long-term memory with minimum effort.

### Active retrieval > passive review
Studies on flashcards consistently show an advantage of retrieval practice (forcing yourself to recall) over passive re-reading. This is the "retrieval practice effect" (RPE). The act of struggling to remember strengthens the memory.

### A few encounters aren't enough
Research repeatedly emphasizes that vocabulary requires many encounters across different contexts before it sticks. Don't expect 2–3 exposures to do it.

### For speaking specifically
Vocab cards alone don't produce fluency. The research points to:
- **Productive practice** — saying words out loud, using them in sentences
- **Lexical chunks / phrases** — learning multi-word units (not just single words) transfers better to speech
- **Output under pressure** — conversation forces active recall in real time, which forges stronger memory links than solitary study

### Frequency-ordered > thematic
Learning the top 1,000–2,000 most-used words gives huge real-world coverage. Thematic lists ("animals," "kitchen items") waste early effort on words you'll rarely use.

## App vs physical cards

| | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **App (Anki, etc.)** | Automatic spaced repetition, audio support, syncs across devices, scales to thousands of cards, free decks available | Learning curve, can feel impersonal |
| **Physical cards** | Writing them aids encoding, tactile, no screen | Manual scheduling (Leitner system) breaks down past ~50 cards, no audio, time-consuming to make |

**Verdict:** Apps win for any serious vocabulary load. Physical cards have one real benefit — handwriting them yourself helps encoding. A good hybrid is to handwrite once, then use an app for review.

### App recommendations
- **Anki** — gold standard. Free on desktop and Android, $25 one-time on iOS. Open-source, proven algorithm, huge community deck library. Steeper learning curve.
- **Quizlet** — friendlier UI, but free tier is limited and algorithm is weaker.

For Armenian, Anki is the clear pick because community decks already exist.

## A practical routine

1. **Install Anki** (desktop + phone, sync them).
2. **Pick a frequency-ordered deck** for your Armenian variant, 1,000–2,000 words.
3. **Daily: 10–20 new cards + reviews.** ~15–20 min. Consistency beats intensity.
4. **Make cards bidirectional** — Armenian→English AND English→Armenian. The English→Armenian direction is what builds speaking recall.
5. **Use phrase cards too**, not just single words. Lexical chunks transfer to speech better.
6. **Add audio.** Either deck-included or pulled from Forvo. Critical for pronunciation.
7. **Pair with output.** This is non-negotiable for speaking:
   - Weekly italki tutor session (cheap, native speakers)
   - Daily shadowing — listen to Armenian audio, repeat aloud immediately
   - Speak the cards aloud as you review them, not silently
