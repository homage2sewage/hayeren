# Determinate (definite) article in Eastern Armenian

Eastern Armenian marks **definiteness via a suffix** (`-ը` or `-ն`)
on the noun, not via a preceding word like English's `the`. [#1] [#2]
Indefiniteness is marked by the preposed `մի` "one / a" or, more
commonly, by the absence of any marker (zero-marking). [#4]

This is **the** classical Russian-L1 contrast — Russian has no
articles at all. [#2]

## The basic rule

> **Sakayan [#1]**: "Armenian expresses such determination by
> affixing the definite article `-ը` or `-ն` to the noun. The
> selection of `-ը` or `-ն` depends on the phonetic environment."

> **Parnasyan [#2]**: "Unlike Russian, in Armenian
> definiteness/indefiniteness of nouns is expressed by specialized
> grammatical markers. Definiteness is expressed by articles ը and ն."

The two-suffix system:

| condition | suffix | example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| noun ends in consonant, next word also starts with a consonant | `-ը` | գիրք**ը** "the book" |
| noun ends in vowel | `-ն` | երեխա**ն** "the child" |
| noun ends in consonant, next word starts with a vowel | `-ն` | գիրք**ն** ընկած է "the book is fallen" |

Pronunciation: `-ը` is the schwa /ə/, often barely audible in fast
speech; `-ն` is the voiced alveolar /n/.

## Indefinite article: `մի`

Tioyan introduces `մի` early (Lesson 8): [#4]

> **Tioyan [#4]**: *"Неопределенный артикль `մի`"* — "the indefinite
> article `մի`."

Etymologically `մի` is the numeral "one." Functionally it acts as an
indefinite article ("a / an"), placed *before* the noun:

- **մի գիրք** "a book" (one specific but unidentified book)
- **գիրք** (bare) "[a] book" (generic; no marker)

The bare form is also indefinite — `մի` is used when the speaker
wants to emphasize the singular/one nature, or in narratives.

## What this topic is *not*

- **Possessive suffixes** (`-ս`, `-դ`, `-ը` 3sg) are a parallel system
  that interacts with the article: a noun with a possessive doesn't
  take the definite article (`գիրքս` "my book" already definite, no
  additional `-ը`). Possessives are a separate topic.
- **Demonstratives** (`այս/էս`, `այդ/էդ`, `այն/էն` "this / that /
  yon") interact with definiteness but are a different surface
  device. Separate topic.
- **Word-formation** uses of `-ը` (e.g. forming participial nouns
  via infinitive + `-ը`) are derivational, not inflectional
  definiteness. Mention only.

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: Armenian articles are **suffixes, not
separate words**. `the book` ≈ `գիրքը` (one word). Don't try to
reproduce the English structure — there's no separate article to
place. The phonetic-environment rule (`-ը` vs `-ն`) has no English
analogue; treat it as a fixed pattern to memorise. The indefinite
`մի` is closer to English `a/an` since it precedes the noun, but
it's *optional* in many contexts where English requires `a/an`.

**For a Russian L1**: this is the **biggest single grammatical
addition** to your mental model — Russian has no articles. [#2]
Every noun in Armenian discourse must be considered: definite (with
`-ը`/`-ն`) or indefinite (bare or with `մի`)? Russian uses word
order and emphasis to convey definiteness; Armenian uses
morphology. You'll initially under-produce articles (because Russian
has none); the literary norm requires them on subjects "as a
rule" [#1].

The Russian intuition "Russian uses *этот* / *тот* / nothing" is
similar in *function* to Armenian's `այս` / `այդ` / `այն` (`էս` /
`էդ` / `էն` colloquially) — but those are demonstratives, not
articles. The article system is *in addition*: a noun can be
definite-articulated AND demonstrative-marked simultaneously.
