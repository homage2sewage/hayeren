# 1sg aorist subjunctive as offer / permission-question

Armenian's 1st-person singular **aorist subjunctive** (Sakayan
terminology; the bare-stem subjunctive future of
`topics/morphology/subjunctive_mood.md`) doubles as a pragmatic
speech-act marker. With question intonation (`՞` on the verb's
stressed syllable), it signals **speaker-side offer** ("shall I do
X?"), **permission-question** ("may I do X?"), or
**self-deliberation** ("shall I X or Y?") — one form, three
illocutions.

## Form

The morphology is independently treated in
`topics/morphology/subjunctive_mood.md` § "Subjunctive future." For
this topic only the 1sg cell matters:

| class | infinitive | 1sg subj | negated |
|-------|------------|----------|---------|
| I (-ել) | բերել "bring" | բերեմ | չբերեմ |
| I (-ել) | փակել "close" | փակեմ | չփակեմ |
| I (-ել) | ասել "say" | ասեմ | չասեմ |
| II (-ալ) | տալ "give" | տամ | չտամ |
| II (-ալ) | գնալ "go" | գնամ | չգնամ |

Sakayan presents the `տալ` paradigm with the `չ-`-negation forms
inline [#1]:

> `(չ)տամ, (չ)տաս, (չ)տա, (չ)տանք, (չ)տաք, (չ)տան`

## Three illocutions, one form

### 1. Offer (speaker-side)

Sakayan p189 [#2]:

> `Սուրճ բերե՞մ։`
> "Shall I bring coffee?"

To make an offer, just take the 1sg subjunctive and add `՞` — no
`ես`, no auxiliary, no particle. `Տոպրակ տա՞մ։` "shall I give
(you) a bag?" (the cashier-context that motivated this topic; not
in the source corpus — see gaps) works the same way as
`Սուրճ բերե՞մ։`. The offer is the unmarked, default reading when
the form appears sentence-initially with question marking and a
plausible deliverable as object.

### 2. Permission-question (self-directed)

Parnasyan p258 [#4]:

> `բոլորը գնացել են: ես էլ գնա՞մ։ Все ушли. Можно и мне уйти?`

The Russian gloss is the disambiguator: `можно и мне уйти?` "may I
leave too?" — a permission-particle (`можно`) + dative-of-experiencer
(`мне`) + infinitive. Armenian collapses all three into the bare
subjunctive form, with optional `ես էլ` ("I too") for emphasis.

### 3. Self-deliberation

Sakayan p337 [#3]:

> `Գնա՞մ, թե՞ մնամ։`
> "Shall I go, or stay?"

The deliberation reading falls out when the offer is *paired with an
alternative* (`թե` "or"). No interlocutor needs to be addressed; the
speaker is weighing options. Note the second `՞` lands on `թե`
itself, marking the alternative as in scope.

## Pedagogical anchor

Tioyan (p217) teaches the pattern explicitly with Russian glosses
[#5]:

> `— Ասե՞մ: — Сказать? Կարդա՞մ: Прочесть? Գնա՞մ: Уйти?`

The Russian renderings — bare infinitives plus question marks — are
the cleanest cross-linguistic gloss. Russian needs four pieces
(particle `можно` / dative experiencer / infinitive / `?`); Armenian
needs one (the subjunctive form) plus the orthographic question
mark.

## Translation table

| Armenian | English | Russian | speech-act |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Սուրճ բերե՞մ։` (sak. p189) | Shall I bring coffee? | Кофе принести? | offer |
| `Տոպրակ տա՞մ։` (not in corpus — see gaps) | Shall I give (you) a bag? | Пакет дать? | offer (cashier-context) |
| `ես էլ գնա՞մ։` (parn. p258) | Shall I go too? | Можно и мне уйти? | permission |
| `Գնա՞մ, թե՞ մնամ։` (sak. p337) | Shall I go or stay? | Уйти или остаться? | deliberation |
| `Կարդա՞մ։` (tio. p217) | Shall I read? | Прочесть? | offer / permission |

Russian can optionally add `мне` for the deliberation /
permission reading (`(Мне) уйти или остаться?`); Armenian never
needs an overt dative-of-experiencer in any of these.

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: the closest English equivalent is the
"shall I…?" construction (still alive in offers and deliberations
even where "shall" is otherwise vestigial). Armenian's form covers
the additional "may I…?" / "let me…" senses that English splits
across separate constructions.

**For a Russian L1**: Russian uses three distinct strategies for
what Armenian collapses into one form — `(мне) <infinitive>?` for
self-deliberation, `можно (мне) <infinitive>?` for permission,
`принести / сделать?` (bare infinitive question) for an offer.
Armenian's economy is the noteworthy structural fact.

**vs. `կ-`-future (`կտամ` "I'll give")**: the false-friend a learner
will trip on first is the minimal pair

> `Տոպրակ տա՞մ։` — "shall I give (you) a bag?" (offer; speaker
> checking)
> `Տոպրակ կտամ։` — "I'll give (you) a bag." (promise; speaker
> committing)

Same verb stem, same 1sg-personal ending; the `կ-` prefix flips the
illocution from offer/permission to assertion-of-future-act. The
`կ-`-prefixed form is *indicative future*, not subjunctive.
Ghamoyan p67's threat-context `-Ընենց կտամ, որ…` ("I'll hit (you)
so that…") is the same construction in a marked register —
illustrative of how far the commitment reading can be pushed.

## Pragmatic / register notes

- The offer reading often (but not always) implies an interlocutor
  benefit (`Սուրճ բերե՞մ` ≈ "do you want me to bring coffee?"). When
  the implied benefit is the speaker's own (`գնա՞մ` "may I leave?"),
  the same form reads as permission-question. Disambiguation is
  contextual, not morphological.
- Negation (`չ-`) on the form gives the "should I not / shall I
  refrain from" reading. Useful when you've started to offer and
  want to check: `Չասե՞մ։` "should I not say it (after all)?" —
  speaker hesitating before delivering. Marked, contextual.
- The form is **register-flexible**: it appears in Sakayan
  (literary-standard textbook), Parnasyan (didactic-reading
  context), and Tioyan (didactic exposition). Ghamoyan's colloquial
  corpus does not attest it on the passages surfaced by this walk —
  whether that's a real register gap or a corpus-coverage gap is
  open (see gaps).
