# Expressive vocabulary in Yerevan colloquial

The expressive register — cursing, swearing, blessings, strong-
emotion vocabulary — has two layers in Yerevan colloquial
speech: an **Armenian-native** layer (productive curse-and-
blessing templates, attested in ghamoyan) and a **Russian-loan**
layer (productive code-switching pattern that incorporates
Russian expressive roots into Armenian morphology). Both layers
coexist; speakers move between them.

The Russian-loan layer specifically is **not corpus-attested in
the workspace** and is documented here from project knowledge —
the sources we cite (the four books) deliberately exclude
profanity by genre. The Armenian-native layer is attested in
ghamoyan p46, p60, p67. Where claims rest on book vs. project
knowledge, the body marks the distinction.

## Armenian-native curse formulas

> **Ghamoyan [#1]**: *Հերդ էլ անիծած. Հողեմ գլուխդ. Գյոռբեգյոռ
> ըլնես. Ապրի...*

ghamoyan's p46 inventory. Each is a semi-fixed template, not a
single lexeme:

| formula | literal | reading |
|---|---|---|
| `Հերդ էլ անիծած` | "your-father even cursed" | "may your father (also) be cursed" |
| `Հերն էլ անիծած` [#4] | "the-father even cursed" | variant: addressee-neutral form |
| `Հողեմ գլուխդ` | "I-bury-fut your-head" | "I'll bury your head (in the ground)" — strong threat |
| `Գյոռբեգյոռ ըլնես` | "Gyorbe-gyor be-(subj.)" | "may you be utterly ruined" — reduplicated *գյոռ* "grave" intensifier; uses dialectal `ըլնես` for `լինես` (`topics/morphology/dialectal_lnel.md`) |
| `Ապրի` (counter-formula) | "may-(s)he-live" | blessing counterpart: "may he live" |

The productive template is **possessive-marked head-noun + curse-
verb**. Many curses target a relative (father, mother, brother,
home, head) by addressee-marked possession; the verb is one of
the curse-set (`անիծել` "curse," `հողել` "bury," `ըլնել` "be"
+ negative state, etc.).

The blessing counter-formula `Ապրի` mirrors the structure
positively — same productive template, opposite valence.

## The meta-vocabulary

> **Ghamoyan [#2]**: *հայհոյական*

`հայհոյական` "abusive, profane" is the standard Armenian
adjective for the register; `հայհոյանք` is the noun "swearing,
abuse, curse"; `հայհոյել` is the verb "to swear, to curse." All
literary, not register-marked themselves — they describe the
phenomenon from outside.

> **Ghamoyan [#3]**: *հայհոյանք-քֆուր*

The synonymy chain pairs `հայհոյանք` (literary) with `քֆուր`
(colloquial / loan-influenced). `քֆուր` likely euphemizes /
phonologically reshapes a Russian expressive root, though
ghamoyan doesn't formalize this. See `gaps:` for etymology
follow-up.

### The productive frame

A widely-heard Yerevan-colloquial pattern: a Russian
expressive root gets truncated/adapted into an adverbial-state
form, then combined with the Armenian aorist (or other tensed
form) of `գալ` "to come":

| Yerevan colloquial | structural parse | gloss |
|---|---|---|
| `Ohuet eka` / `օհուետ էկա` | Russian *охуеть*-truncation + 1sg aorist of `գալ` (`էկա` = colloquial `եկա`) | "I went stunned / lost it / wtf-ed" |
| `Ofiget eka` / `օֆիգետ էկա` | Russian *офигеть*-truncation + same | softer variant: "I was amazed" |
| (similar productivity) | various Russian state-change roots + `գալ` | "I went into [state]" |

The pattern parallels — but is **not equivalent to** — the
dative-experiencer `դուր գալ`/`քուն գալ` family
(`topics/syntax/dative_experiencer.md`). Both use `գալ` as the
predicate and a state-noun as the subject-of-state. The
difference: native dative-experiencer takes the experiencer in
dative (`Ինձ քուն է գալիս`), while the Russian-loan-expressive
pattern uses 1sg subject + aorist (`Ես ohuet էկա` / often just
`Ohuet էկա` without the pronoun). Closer to the regular motion-
verb structure of `դուրս գալ` "to come out" (`topics/syntax/
dative_experiencer.md` § "the near-homograph trap"), which
takes nom. subject + ablative source — same base argument
shape.

### Common standalone forms

Russian expressive vocabulary also enters Yerevan speech as
standalone interjections / discourse particles, with no
Armenian morphology applied:

| form | source | usage |
|---|---|---|
| `բլյադ` / `bldj` | Russian *блядь* | discourse-particle filler / emphasis (similar to English "damn" used for emphasis) |
| `պիզդեց` / `pizdets` | Russian *пиздец* | exclamation: "what a disaster" / "f-ed up" |
| `ohuet` | Russian *охуеть* (infinitive as exclamation) | exclamation: "wow / no way / wtf" |
| `nahuy` / `nahuy gnac` | Russian *нахуй* | dismissal / "go to hell" |

These are **register-marked male-coded informal**; in mixed
company / formal contexts speakers code-switch back to native
Armenian forms (or simply soften — ghamoyan's `քֆուր`-type
euphemism).

### Phonological adaptation

Russian expressives entering Armenian undergo predictable
adaptation:

- **Truncation of verbal endings.** `охуеть` (full infinitive)
  → `ohuet` (the bare stem, treated as adverbial state).
- **Vowel orthography reshape.** Russian `о` → Armenian `օ` (or
  `о` in Cyrillic transliteration); Russian `у` → Armenian `ու`.
- **Final-consonant treatment.** Soft signs (`ь`) drop; final
  voiced obstruents may devoice per Armenian phonotactics.
- **Stress.** Native Russian stress patterns generally don't
  survive — Armenian stress is word-final by default, so the
  borrowed item gets reanalysed accordingly.

These claims are pre-training-prior level; not workspace-
validated.

## Cross-linguistic register choice

When does a Yerevan speaker choose Armenian-native vs. Russian-
loan curse vocabulary? Project-knowledge hypotheses:

- **Generation.** Older / literary speakers prefer Armenian-
  native (`Հերդ էլ անիծած`); younger / urban-male preference
  shifts toward Russian-loan (`pizdets`, `bldj`).
- **Intensity.** Russian-loan items often carry stronger
  vulgarity perception, possibly because of the *informational
  distance* of the foreign root — the speaker can deploy a
  strong word that's "not really" Armenian, deflecting some
  social cost.
- **Context.** Mixed-script social media / chat tends to
  Russian-loan; spoken family contexts tend to Armenian-native.
- **Audience-design.** Speakers calibrate to listener: more
  Armenian-native with non-Russian-speakers, more Russian-loan
  with bilingual peers.

These are hypotheses, not verified. Worth a sociolinguistic
study; flagged as `gaps:` above.

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: the trap here is similar to the broader
code-switching trap (`topics/lexicon/code_switching_with_
russian.md`) — Yerevan speakers can produce a sentence whose
swears are Russian-derived but whose syntactic frame is
purely Armenian. Don't try to parse `Ohuet էկա` as if it were
all-Russian or all-Armenian; it's neither, and the productive
frame is what makes it grammatical in Yerevan colloquial.

**For a Russian L1**: the Russian-loan layer is partially
familiar — you'll recognise the roots (*охуел*, *пиздец*,
*блядь*) — but the **constructions** are Armenian. `Ohuet
эка` parsed as Russian doesn't make sense (`эка` isn't a
Russian word); parsed as Armenian-with-Russian-loan it does
(`էկա` = 1sg aorist of `գալ`). The Russian L1 reader
recognises the lexical content but has to apply Armenian
syntax. Mirror image of the English L1 case.

For both: the rule "if a sentence has Russian-recognisable
*roots* but Armenian-script morphology, expect a code-switched
construction" is the diagnostic.
