# Colloquial 3sg copula and auxiliary `ա`

In Yerevan colloquial speech, the literary 3rd-person-singular form
`է` is replaced by `ա`. The substitution applies in two places: as
the **linker (հանգույց / copula) of compound predicates**, and as
the **auxiliary in analytic verb forms**. [#1] This is the single
most recognisable register marker of Yerevan colloquial speech —
present in nearly every spontaneous third-person sentence.

## What gets substituted

Per Ghamoyan's canonical statement [#1]:

> "In folk-colloquial language, `ա` is used as a linker
> (հանգույց) with the third person of compound predicates… in
> simple [analytic] predicates, `ա` replaces the auxiliary verb `է`."

| function | literary | colloquial |
|----------|----------|------------|
| copula (3sg) | մեծ **է** "is big"   | մեծ **ա**   |
| copula (3sg) | լավն **է** "is good" | լավն **ա**  |
| copula (3sg) | կանաչ **է** "is green" | կանաչ **ա** |
| present aux  | գրում **է** "s/he writes" | գրում **ա** |
| present aux  | գալիս **է** "s/he comes"  | գալիս **ա** |
| present aux  | ուտում **է** "s/he eats"  | ուտում **ա** |

Other persons (1sg `եմ`, 2sg `ես`, 1pl `ենք`, 2pl `եք`, 3pl `են`)
are unaffected by the substitution as ghamoyan describes it.

## Scope

Ghamoyan's framing covers two functions of `է`:

1. **Copular `է`**, joining a subject to a non-verbal predicate
   (adjective, noun, locative phrase). This is what ghamoyan calls
   *բաղադրյալ ստորոգյալի հանգույց* "the linker of a compound
   predicate."
2. **Auxiliary `է`** in analytic tenses (present, perfect, future,
   etc.) where the participle carries lexical content and the
   auxiliary carries person/number. This is what ghamoyan calls
   *պարզ ստորոգյալ* "simple predicate" — the analytic verb form
   acting as a single grammatical predicate.

Ghamoyan documents both, with examples for each. [#1]

## What ghamoyan does *not* settle

The book restricts itself to present-tense / copular `է`. Several
adjacent questions remain open (see frontmatter `gaps:`):

- Whether past `էր` shifts colloquially (e.g. to `ա էր`, `էր → ար`,
  or unchanged).
- Whether other persons have any colloquial-only forms.
- The etymology / status of `ա` — sound change vs analogy vs dialect
  borrowing.

These would benefit from either fieldwork data or a typological
reference grammar (Dum-Tragut 2009 likely treats `ա` directly).

## Implication for present_tense paradigms

The literary 3sg form `գրում է` should be presented alongside the
colloquial `գրում ա` whenever register is in scope. See
`topics/morphology/present_tense.md` for the literary paradigm; this
topic is its colloquial counterpart for 3sg only.

For Anki cards, an `ա`-form variant of the 3sg present cell is
worth surfacing as a separate "register: colloquial" card — easy to
generate from the existing paradigm by mechanical substitution
(`է → ա` in the 3sg cell only).

## Pro-drop and elision

The colloquial `ա` is itself eligible for the broader pattern of
auxiliary elision documented in
`topics/syntax/pro_drop.md` — i.e., even `ա` can drop in
zero-copula sentences (`Դու` խենթ ու խելառ` "you (are) crazy and
silly"). The substitution and the elision are independent
phenomena that compose: `ա` is the form *if* a 3sg copula/auxiliary
is overt at all.

## Contrastive notes

**For an English L1**: the closest mental model is the
"is/are → 's/re" contraction in casual English (`it's big`,
`they're coming`) — except Armenian's substitution is
phonologically more drastic (different vowel, different
morphological host) and applies *only* to 3sg, not other persons.
Don't try to map word-for-word; recognise `ա` as a register tell
and produce it when imitating colloquial speech.

**For a Russian L1**: Russian has no copula in present tense
(zero-copula: `он умный`), so there's no direct `ա ↔ є/есть`
analogue. But Russian does have register-specific 3sg verb forms
in some contexts (regional / non-standard `мабуть`, dialect
endings). The pattern of "form X is literary, form Y is
colloquial *for the same grammatical slot*" is structurally
familiar from Russian's literary-vs-spoken split. The Yerevan
`ա ↔ է` distinction is sharper and more frequent than any single
Russian pair, but the cognitive template is the same.
