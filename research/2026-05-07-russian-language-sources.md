# Russian-language Armenian sources — candidate list

**Date**: 2026-05-07
**Trigger**: explicit user request to add a Russian-language textbook
or contrastive grammar — to complement Sakayan (English-side
contrast) and ghamoyan (in-Armenian descriptive).
**Status**: candidate list with brief assessments; acquisition pending
user decision.

## Why a Russian-language source

Three reasons specific to the project's current state:

1. **Russian-L1 contrastive notes** are already present in every
   topic file but are agent/user-paraphrased rather than book-
   grounded. A Russian-side source converts those notes from "vibes"
   to citations.
2. **A different theoretical lens.** Soviet-era Russian-language
   Armenian linguistics has a distinct typological tradition from
   Western descriptive grammars; the cross-tradition contrast is
   itself diagnostic.
3. **Triangulation.** With Sakayan (English, prescriptive-pedagogical),
   ghamoyan (Armenian, descriptive-colloquial), and a Russian-
   contrastive source, every existing topic gets at least three
   independent viewpoints — much harder to misread any single book's
   framing as truth.

## Candidate 1 (top recommendation): Parnasyan & Manukyan (1990)

**Title**: *Самоучитель армянского языка* (Armenian Self-Teacher).
**Authors**: Н. А. Парнасян, Ж. К. Манукян.
**Publisher / year**: Луйс, Yerevan, 1990. 431 pp.

**Why this**: explicitly framed as Armenian-Russian contrastive.
Per the publisher's blurb, the textbook "compares with Russian,
paying special attention to correspondences and differences in
phonetic and grammatical structure of both languages." Structure:
introduction, phonetic course (5 lessons), main course (15 lessons).
Pre-supposes Russian-grammar knowledge at secondary-school level —
i.e., it can use Russian-linguistic terms freely, which is what we
want for citing Russian-L1 framings.

**What it likely buys us**:

- Armenian phonology framed for a Russian-L1 ear (e.g., the
  three-way laryngeal contrast contrasted with Russian's two-way
  voiced/voiceless system).
- Case-system mapping (Armenian's 7 cases ↔ Russian's 6) as a first-
  class topic, not a footnote.
- A Russian-language vocabulary for grammatical phenomena that we
  can fold back into `grammar-terms.md` for citations.
- A second prescriptive-pedagogical voice on the literary norm,
  potentially flipping more topics from `multi-attested` to
  triangulated 3-source coverage.

**Risks / caveats**:

- 1990 publication: predates major spelling reform discussions and
  newer pedagogical conventions. Eastern Armenian's literary norm
  is mostly stable across that span, but specific examples may show
  Soviet-era register choices.
- Soviet-era linguistic tradition tends toward heavy formalism;
  some claims may be framed in ways that need translation into
  modern typological vocabulary.
- 431 pages is large; full extraction will take effort comparable
  to Sakayan's.

**Sources**: book entry on `obuchalka.org` and `studmed.ru`; full
text scanned and available on `babylonians.narod.ru`. Google Books
preview at https://books.google.com/books/about/Самоучитель_армянско.html?hl=ru&id=Cbevz24vtbsC

## Candidate 2: Markosyan (2003 / 2013)

**Title**: *Կռունկ Հայաստանի (Крунк Айастани): Учебник армянского
языка*.
**Author**: Аида Суреновна Маркосян.
**Publisher / year**: Баласс, Moscow, 2003 (1st ed.); 2013 expanded
edition with introductory course. 192+ pp.

**Why this**: more recent than Parnasyan. Author is a Yerevan-
trained linguist and PhD, currently Professor at Moscow City
Pedagogical University. The book is in the standard Russian-
pedagogical-grammar tradition, with a "concise systematic
description" of Armenian phonetics and grammar plus an Armenian-
Russian study dictionary.

**Why not first pick**: less explicitly contrastive than Parnasyan
— described as a structured pedagogy of Armenian, not as a
comparative text. Russian framing is implicit (target audience
Russian-speaking) rather than methodological.

**Sources**: book entries on `armeniaonline.ru`, `aybuben.com`, and
the Russian National Library catalogue (NEB).

## Candidate 3: Garibyan (2017)

**Title**: *Учим армянский самостоятельно*.
**Author**: Дж. А. Гарибян.

**Why mention**: Garibyan is a respected Armenian linguist with
multiple scholarly works on Armenian etymology and grammar. This
particular title is a popular self-study but the author's
linguistic credibility lifts it above other "popular" titles.

**Why not first pick**: lower density of explicit contrastive
framing than Parnasyan; the author's *scholarly* works aren't this
book.

**Sources**: `obuchalka.org` and other educational portals.

## Candidate 4 (popular, lowest priority): Stepanyan (2020s)

**Title**: *Армянский язык без репетитора. Самоучитель армянского
языка.*
**Author**: Дарий Степанян.
**Publisher**: AST. ISBN 978-5-17-121136-3.

**Why mention**: most recent of the four; widely available; cheap.

**Why low priority**: a popular self-study book, not academic. Useful
for the Russian-L1 *learner experience* but probably won't add new
linguistic framing beyond what Parnasyan or Markosyan already
provide. Worth keeping in reserve as a third or fourth source if the
project ever needs more pedagogical-tone material.

## Out of scope (this round)

- **Scholarly Russian-language Armenian linguistics** — works by
  Туманян, Аракелян, Ачарян etc. exist in Russian but tend toward
  historical/dialectological rather than synchronic-pedagogical
  frames. Worth a separate research pass if the project ever needs
  diachronic depth.
- **Russian-language Western Armenian** sources — there are some,
  but Western Armenian is currently outside this project's scope.

## Recommendation

**Acquire Parnasyan & Manukyan first.** It directly addresses the
user's request for a Russian-side contrastive source, has the most
density of Russian-Armenian comparison material, and gives the
project a clear third pillar (English-contrastive Sakayan +
Armenian-descriptive ghamoyan + Russian-contrastive Parnasyan).

Markosyan (Candidate 2) is the natural follow-up if Parnasyan's
1990 register feels stale or its formalism feels heavy. The two
together would cover both the Soviet-era and the modern Russian
pedagogical traditions.

After acquisition: a topic-walk of all 6 existing topics against
Parnasyan would be the natural next step (cf. the ghamoyan
integration plan from 2026-05-07). Expect to find:

- corroboration of Sakayan's literary-norm claims (most topics
  flip from `multi-attested` (sakayan + ghamoyan) to fully
  triangulated)
- Russian-L1-framed contrastive notes that can replace agent-
  paraphrased ones
- new topics on the Armenian↔Russian case correspondence (already
  partly in `grammar-terms.md`, but could deserve standalone topics
  per case).
